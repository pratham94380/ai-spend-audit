# Go-To-Market Strategy

## Target Audience

Primary users:

- startup founders
- engineering managers
- AI-heavy SaaS teams
- developer-first startups
- companies rapidly adopting AI tooling

The ideal early customer is:
- 5–50 person startup
- actively paying for multiple AI subscriptions
- cost-sensitive but productivity-focused
- experimenting heavily with AI workflows

---

# Core Problem

Most startups:
- accumulate overlapping AI subscriptions
- move to enterprise plans too early
- lack visibility into actual AI tooling ROI
- struggle to benchmark spend efficiency

AI tooling adoption is increasing faster than spend optimization practices.

This creates a strong opportunity for:
- AI cost visibility
- tooling consolidation
- spend optimization recommendations

---

# Positioning

AI Spend Audit positions itself as:

> “The easiest way for startups to understand whether they are overspending on AI tooling.”

The product is intentionally:
- lightweight
- fast
- no-login
- instantly useful

This reduces friction and improves shareability.

---

# Initial Distribution Strategy

## 1. Founder Communities

Primary channels:
- Reddit startup communities
- Indie Hackers
- Hacker News
- AI engineering communities
- Twitter/X founder audience

Content examples:
- “We audited 50 startup AI stacks — most were overspending.”
- “Startups are wasting thousands yearly on overlapping AI tools.”

These are designed to trigger curiosity and self-evaluation.

---

## 2. Product-Led Growth

The audit itself is shareable.

Users discovering large savings opportunities are likely to:
- share screenshots
- discuss findings internally
- forward audits to decision makers

This creates lightweight virality loops.

---

## 3. SEO Strategy

Potential long-tail SEO pages:

- “ChatGPT Team vs Enterprise pricing”
- “Best AI tools for startups”
- “AI SaaS cost calculator”
- “Claude vs ChatGPT pricing comparison”
- “How much should startups spend on AI tools?”

These pages align naturally with audit intent.

# Biggest GTM Risk

The biggest risk is:
- users perceiving the product as a simple calculator instead of a valuable optimization platform

To overcome this:
- recommendations must feel actionable
- insights must feel trustworthy
- savings opportunities must feel meaningful

The product succeeds only if users feel:
> “This showed me something I didn’t already know.”
